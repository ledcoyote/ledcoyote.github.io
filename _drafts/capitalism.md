A brief primer on my current understanding of economics, the role of its various
inputs, and capitalism. Mostly here to organize my thoughts; I don't claim
anything original here.

# Basic Terms

## Goods

Economics is all about what we call "goods." These are sometimes physical,
sometimes intangible _things_ that flow through society. Humans seek out goods
for their own ends, whether physiological in the case of foodstuffs, aesthetic
in the case of arts, or whatever other personal reason may exist.

## Value

At the heart of all economic thinking throughout history is that of "value." Its
sources and definition have been debated. Some have posited that the value of a
good derives from the labor that went into its production. Others claim that it
comes from an entirely subjective process of ordering individual preferences.

Much of the confusion comes from a conflation of values with prices. The price
of a good is that which one must give up in order to obtain it. In most
economies today prices are given in monetary terms, that is, how much currency
must one give up to obtain the good in question.

# The Inputs

At the very base of things, an society creates goods by extracting raw materials
and labor from the land and workers in the system. These raw materials and labor
flow into economic centers and are transformed by intellect into artifacts that
can serve the needs of humans. "Services" are the direct application of labor to
value creation for a human.

An economic center, or "firm," is a place where labor and materials are brought
together to create something of greater value than its inputs. It creates that
value through the application of intellect. The output of a firm might be goods
or services for direct consumption, or it might be intermediate goods that flow
into another firm for further transformation.

## Capital

Capital is the orienting and direction-making force in an economic system. In a
monetary economic system with a sophisticated financial system, capital has the
ability to move fluidly from firm to firm with minimal friction.

Capital is able to effect this function by adjusting its outlays into rents and
wages. When wages are reduced for a labor force, or shut off altogether in the
case of firing, it goes elsewhere in search of income to support the workers and
their dependents. When increased through raises or hiring, labor flows to the
economic center. The same process applies to rents. However, the readjustment
cannot occur instantaneously. When transitioning between firms, labor may remain
unemployed, land may lie fallow, or capital may be idle.
